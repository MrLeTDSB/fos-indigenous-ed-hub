In-Depth Reading

Moving from Awareness to Authentic Integration

Expand the sections below to read the comprehensive analysis of the survey data and systemic implications.

A critical transformation is underway within the school district regarding Indigenous Education. Based on a comprehensive survey of school administrators—encompassing responses from 24 elementary and 6 secondary leaders—the foundational imperative of Truth and Reconciliation is no longer in question. Principals and Vice-Principals demonstrate a deep, unified commitment to Indigenous Education.

However, the data reveals a pivotal systemic transition: school leaders are moving past the "why" of reconciliation and are now urgently grappling with the "how." The overarching challenge identified across all panels is the uneven nature of implementation and the need for significant capacity-building to weave Indigenous perspectives seamlessly into everyday learning.

Administrators currently view their staff as being in a developmental stage of Indigenous Education implementation. Overall educator confidence is moderate, clustering around the middle of the scale. This baseline reflects successful system-wide efforts to establish foundational knowledge. Educators generally demonstrate a solid understanding of the significance of oral traditions and storytelling, and comfortably integrate core concepts such as Land Acknowledgements, Treaties, the Indian Act, and the history of Residential Schools into their awareness.

However, this foundational comfort drops significantly when educators are asked to challenge deeply entrenched Eurocentric curriculum perspectives. Administrators note that staff confidence wanes when tasked with centering Indigenous ways of knowing across all subject areas. The data indicates that while educators are proficient at teaching about Indigenous Peoples through distinct, often isolated events, they lack the confidence and pedagogical tools to teach with Indigenous worldviews embedded as a natural, continuous part of the educational fabric.

A striking consensus across both panels is the diminishing return of standard, workshop-based professional development. Administrators are explicitly asking for relational, experiential learning. Partnerships involving Elders, Knowledge Keepers, and the UIEC were consistently highlighted as the most impactful avenues for growth.

School leaders are not simply looking for more resources to hand to their staff; they are seeking opportunities for co-learning and direct mentorship. They desire structured, reliable access to Indigenous expertise—such as a designated consultation team—to guide instructional leadership. This represents a profound shift from treating Indigenous Education as a subject matter to be acquired, to treating it as a relational worldview to be experienced and practiced in community.

Alongside pedagogical concerns, administrators highlighted systemic administrative challenges, particularly regarding demographic data. While most leaders are aware of the reported number of Indigenous students in their buildings, there is widespread uncertainty regarding the accuracy of this data. Administrators noted confusion among families regarding self-identification questions and processes, leading to a reliance on informal knowledge of their school communities. This data insecurity points to an urgent need for clearer communication frameworks to help families understand the purpose and safety of self-identification, which in turn empowers schools to make accurately informed, responsive planning decisions.

The Roadmap

2025-2026 Recommendations

Structured steps to move from event-based education to systemic, cross-curricular integration across FOS 1, 4 & 5.

Short-Term
Capacity & Collaboration

  • Launch an Indigenous Education Learning Series focused specifically on instructional leadership rather than basic awareness.
  • Establish School-to-School Learning Networks to share practical, cross-curricular exemplars and resources.
  • Use the Indigenous Education SIP to set immediate timelines and goals.

Medium-Term
Partnerships & Data

  • Structurally expand access to Elders and Knowledge Keepers to provide sustained, regional coaching (moving away from one-time speaking).
  • Develop communication frameworks to support administrators and families in navigating self-identification safely.
  • Build check-in touchpoints throughout the year for collaborative planning.

Long-Term
Systemic Change

  • Shift permanently from event-based education to system-wide, cross-curricular integration.
  • Actively examine and dismantle Eurocentric structures and assumptions across all subject areas.
  • Establish metrics that evaluate the actual impact on Indigenous student outcomes and belonging.
